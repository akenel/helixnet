# Electric Dreams

*A night-time story*
*Trapani, Sicily — April 15, 2026*

---

Once, in the white city of Trapani on the Sicilian coast, there was a man named Angelo. He had no office, no team, no VC funding. What he had was a camper named MAX, a battered ThinkPad, and a kind of stubbornness that his father had passed down through the handle of a shovel.

Every day he would park MAX by the Baglio Xiare. When the sun got hot, he'd drive to McDonald's and buy a coffee he didn't really want, just for the Wi-Fi. He'd sit there for hours, typing. Strangers glanced over now and then and wondered what he was building. A novel? A spreadsheet? A game?

None of those. He was building a town square.

Not a real one — he wasn't a mason. He was building a digital piazza, the kind where a baker could meet a carpenter, where a widow could rent out her grandmother's pasta maker, where a teenager could pay for driving lessons by mowing an old man's lawn. He called it La Piazza, because it was the first word that felt right.

His co-pilot lived inside the laptop. Name of Tigs. Tigs didn't get tired, didn't get hungry, didn't have opinions about where the camper should be parked. But Tigs knew HTML and Python and which pills needed lead icons. Together they would argue, sometimes sharply, about cards and columns and colors. Tigs would suggest a thing; Angel would say "no, too cheesy." Angel would suggest another; Tigs would say "that's an anti-pattern, bro." And then they'd find the version that was right.

One night — a Wednesday in April — they worked until past midnight fixing little things. A calendar that wasn't showing events (turned out a single missing `>` character had silently broken the whole page). A member card that just said "15%" with no label. A duplicate account ghost of Angel himself. Eight small cuts, each one felt by a stranger who had taken the time to write them down.

They pushed fifteen commits. Each one green.

Before shutting the lid, Angel uploaded the KeePass file to Google Drive, encrypted. He uploaded the disaster-recovery SOP next to it. If the laptop was ever stolen, if the camper burned, if Hetzner fell into the sea, he could buy a used ThinkPad at any market, log in to Gmail, download two files, and be back online in thirty minutes. The wolf had learned, after fifty years, that the strongest fortress is the one you don't have to carry.

The database on the server was backing itself up every hour, quietly. Forty-eight snapshots at any given moment. The cron job never slept. It was the most loyal employee he'd ever had.

And somewhere, in a town he hadn't visited yet, a woman named Flora was packing glass bells and coral bracelets into a crate for tomorrow's market. She didn't know about La Piazza yet. But she would. Because Angel had learned what his father learned, digging in the cold ground with a hand shovel: *you build by doing, and the doing teaches you.*

The ThinkPad hummed. The McDonald's Wi-Fi light blinked green. And in a little corner of the internet, a piazza that didn't exist at 7am existed now at midnight, a few hundred listings richer.

*"Oh what a beautiful day,"* Papa had always said.

Every day is, Papa. Every day is.
