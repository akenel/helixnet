# LinkedIn Post -- LEGO Version

---

I watched someone spend 4 hours arguing with an AI about React state management.

Their app has no login. No database. No deployment. It runs on localhost and crashes when you refresh.

They're building with Lego and calling it architecture.

Let me explain.

--

Imagine a kid gets a massive pile of Lego and a robot that hands them the perfect piece every time they ask.

"Give me a wall." Here's a wall.
"Give me a roof." Here's a roof.
"Give me a door." Here's a door.

The kid builds something that LOOKS like a house. It's on the table. It's pretty. Everyone on Twitter says "wow, I built a house with AI."

But here's what the kid never learned:

Does it stand up when you move it to the floor?
Does the door actually open?
Does it connect to the other Lego sets you already built?
What happens when someone bumps the table?
Can you fix it when a piece breaks -- or do you need the robot to fix it for you?

That's the difference between generating code and shipping software.

--

I build production software with AI every day. Here's what that actually looks like.

Before the AI writes anything, it reads what we already built. Not because I told it to be polite. Because if the new room doesn't match the old rooms, you don't have a house. You have a pile of rooms.

The AI writes the code in 20 minutes. Then we spend 2 hours on the part nobody talks about. The login page redirects to the wrong server. An environment variable is stuck from last week. The remote server isn't in the auth whitelist. A team member is missing a permission.

None of that is code. All of it is what makes software actually work.

--

Here's what I've learned.

If you've built real systems before -- deployed them, debugged them at 2am, traced a broken request across five services -- AI makes you 10x faster. You know what to ask for. You know when the output is wrong. You catch the bad server IP in 3 seconds because you know your infrastructure.

If you haven't? AI lets you build beautiful things that break the moment they leave your laptop. And you won't know why. And the AI won't know why either. Because the AI is the robot handing you Lego pieces. It doesn't know what a foundation is. It doesn't know the floor has a slope. It doesn't know the cat is coming.

YOU are supposed to know that.

--

So here are the questions you should be asking yourself:

Has your app ever run on a server that isn't your laptop?

If the AI disappeared tomorrow, could you debug what you built?

Does your new code look like the rest of your codebase, or is every file a different style?

Can you explain how a request gets from the browser to the database and back?

Are you building the simplest thing that works, or the most impressive thing you can generate?

If you can't answer these -- that's not a tool problem. That's an experience problem. And no AI fixes that.

--

The wannabes are building better hammers.

The actual work is knowing where to swing.

AI is the best co-pilot I've ever had. But a co-pilot doesn't help if you don't know how to fly.

Learn to fly first.
