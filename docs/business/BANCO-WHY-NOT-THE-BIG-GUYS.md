# Banco — "Why mine and not SAP / Odoo / Zoho?"

*Positioning & objection-handling brief. 2026-06-27. The doc you read from — or hand over — when a prospect (or you, at 2am) asks: "Why would Felix buy yours when SAP Business One / Odoo / Zoho exist, have 10,000 features and 24/7 support, and you're one person?"*

## THE ONE-LINE VERDICT

**The question contains a category error, and once you see it, it's your whole defense.** Felix is not choosing between Banco and SAP Business One. He never was — a €15k–50k+ ERP implementation with a 3–6 month consultant project attached was never on the table for a 25-seat shop, and any vendor who says it was is about to sell him a project he'll regret. You're not in a fight with SAP. You're in a fight with **a spreadsheet, a generic foreign POS, and doing nothing** — and that's a fight a tailored, in-person, low-cost tool wins.

Don't compete on feature-count or global SLA theater. You'll lose both and you don't need to win either. Win on **fit, proximity, total cost, and the SOPs** — and be honest about the one real weakness (continuity), then answer it.

---

## 1. THE CATEGORY ERROR (the reframe)

SAP looks like "the big serious thing," so being compared to it feels like losing before you start. But:

- **SAP / Odoo (full ERP) / Dynamics BC don't compete for Felix.** They don't send a salesperson to a CBD micro-shop. They want companies with an IT department and a procurement budget. Felix is, to them, too small to bother with — *on purpose.*
- So "why not SAP?" has a true answer that **wins the room**: *"SAP was never built for a shop your size. If a vendor tells you it is, they're about to sell you a six-month project and a per-seat bill you'll regret. They genuinely don't want a customer this small. I do."*

The fear is real but misplaced. You are not the dinky version of SAP. You are the *correctly-sized thing* SAP refuses to make.

## 2. THE REAL SHELF — who Felix can actually choose from

Sized for a Swiss micro-retailer, the genuine alternatives — and the one thing each one is missing:

| Category | Examples | Strength | What it's missing for Felix |
|----------|----------|----------|------------------------------|
| **Swiss SMB suites** | Bexio, KLARA, Abacus | Swiss-localized, solid accounting, ~CHF 25–100/mo | Accounting-first; thin generic POS. Doesn't know a CBD shop or a dine-in/takeaway VAT split from a hole in the ground. |
| **POS-specific** | SumUp, Square, Lightspeed, Vectron, Tilby, Gastrofix | Easy, cheap to start, good hardware | Foreign-built, generic. Treats CH as "just another country" — fudges the 8.1/2.6 split, no CBD age/compliance, no Treuhänder-grade Z-report. |
| **Generic cloud suites** | Zoho | Broad, cheap, integrated | You're a tiny account in a giant catalog. Support is a ticket queue in another timezone. *Nothing* is shaped to his business. |
| **Full ERP** | SAP Business One, Odoo, MS Dynamics BC | Capable, enterprise-grade | Implementation project + consultant + per-seat licensing. Generic until configured. Not sold to shops this size. |

**The map:** every option misses *at least one* of the four things Felix needs (Swiss-correct VAT/fiscal · CBD compliance · in-person setup · low total cost). **None** of them shows up in his shop and speaks his exact situation. That intersection — the empty one — is where Banco sits. (Same finding as the market brief: the wedge is genuinely empty, defensible on *execution and relationships*, not a technical wall.)

## 3. THE MOAT — and none of it is feature-count

You will never out-feature Odoo. Don't try. You win on what doesn't fit in a feature matrix:

1. **Fit, not features.** The dine-in 8.1% / takeaway 2.6% split is *legally mandatory* for his 25-seat cafe (he exceeds the 20-seat flat-rate cap) and most foreign POS fudge it. CBD age-gating, the Z-report a Swiss Treuhänder will actually sign — built *because you sat in his shop.* No global vendor will make that for a niche this small.
2. **Proximity / accountability.** With SAP, Felix is ticket #84,221. With you, the person who feels the problem is the person who *built the thing* and already knows his shop. Enterprises pay millions trying to buy that closeness back. You give it for free by being small.
3. **TCO, not sticker price.** No per-seat licensing, no implementation consultant, no annual audit true-up. You're cheaper *and* you're the implementer. That's a real number to put on the table.
4. **The SOPs and the stewardship.** You don't sell a database. You sell *his shop running well Monday morning* — software + procedures + AI help. That's the genuinely-yours part (see solo-founder & steward notes), and it's the part no incumbent bundles.

## 4. THE HONEST WEAKNESS — and how you cover it

Pretending the support gap doesn't exist is the 4-star move. Name it, then answer it. (5-star: say what's true, prove the cover.)

### 4a. Bus factor — the *real* fear
One person. If you're sick, hospitalized, or gone, what happens to Felix's till? This is the legitimate objection — and it's about **continuity, not 24/7 phone support.** The answers:

- **Code escrow / data ownership.** Felix owns his data and gets a copy of the system. He is *never* hostage to one person being alive and reachable.
- **Backups with tested restore — already built.** The daily backup-with-restore-drill (dump → restore to throwaway DB → compare → 30-day retention) is literally a continuity story you can *show*, not promise.
- **The steward model.** The long-game answer: you're not the only person who can run it. A steward guild is forming — others can deploy and operate the same system. (See [STEWARD-GUILD-AND-WHY-NOW.md](STEWARD-GUILD-AND-WHY-NOW.md).)

### 4b. "But they have a 24/7 SLA"
Reframe honestly: a 25-seat shop **does not need a global 24/7 SLA** — that's enterprise theater priced for 10,000 users. What Felix needs is *"it works during my open hours and a human picks up."* You can offer that, in writing:

- Defined support **hours** and a guaranteed **response window you can actually keep.** A small kept SLA beats a grand SLA that's really a call-center in another timezone.
- **Offline mode** (the P2 outbox) so a network blip never stops a sale — resilience that doesn't depend on anyone answering a phone.

### 4c. Don't fight where you lose
No 24/7. No 99.99% uptime guarantee for a solo op. No "we have 500 engineers." **Say so.** Choose the customers for whom fit + proximity + price beats those things — micro-retail, hospitality, the CBD niche — and let the enterprises go to SAP. That's not retreat; it's choosing a winnable field.

## 5. THE SCRIPT — what to actually say when asked

**Short version (the skeptic at the counter):**

> "SAP and Odoo are built for companies with an IT department. You don't have one — you have me. They'll sell you a six-month project and a per-seat bill; I'll set up your exact shop, handle your Swiss VAT and CBD rules they don't bother with, and you can call the person who built it. You're not too small for them by accident — they genuinely don't want a shop your size. I do."

**If pushed on support / "what if you get hit by a bus":**

> "Fair question, and I won't bluff it. You're not buying a 500-engineer call center, and you don't need one. You're buying: your shop running Monday, support during your hours with a response window I'll put in writing, your data backed up and test-restored every night, a copy of the system that's yours to keep, and — increasingly — other stewards who can run it if I can't. That's more continuity than being ticket #84,000 in a queue gives you."

**If they name a specific incumbent:**
- *Bexio / KLARA:* "Great accounting. Ask them to handle your dine-in/takeaway VAT split and CBD age-gate. They won't — it's not their shop."
- *Square / SumUp / Lightspeed:* "Easy to start, foreign-built. Ask if their Z-report passes a Swiss Treuhänder and does 8.1/2.6 per line. It doesn't."
- *Zoho:* "Broad and cheap. You'll be a tiny account configuring it yourself, with support in another timezone. I configure it *for* you, for your trade."

---

## 6. WHAT THIS BRIEF IS *NOT* SAYING

- Not saying Banco is more capable than Odoo. It isn't, and capability isn't the axis you win on.
- Not saying the moat is a technical wall. It's execution + relationships + locality — a *motivated* incumbent could localize (per the market brief). The defense is being faster, closer, and cheaper to *this* customer than a giant will ever bother to be.
- Not saying the continuity weakness is solved. Escrow + steward guild are the *plan*; backups are the *proof so far*. Be honest that the guild is still forming.
