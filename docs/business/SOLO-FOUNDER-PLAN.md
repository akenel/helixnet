## The reframe: the bottleneck is money + being alone, not the software

~70% of an engine is built and there's a live customer (Felix/Banco). The classic solo-dev death is
**building forever and never charging.** So the whole plan tilts from **building → selling + delegating.**
Less fun than code; it's the actual job now.

## Phase 0 — Get the first franc (next few weeks)  ← the priority
Felix is live. **Charge him.** Even modestly. **Setup fee + small monthly retainer + hourly for changes**
(the model Angel already described — no per-user, client pays own hosting or our managed markup). The amount
isn't the point; the point is converting "project" → "business" with one real invoice. Until someone pays,
it's a hobby.

**First-franc checklist (Felix):**
- [ ] Close any go-live gap so Banco is genuinely usable day-to-day (closeout/timesheet work in flight — [[banco-closeout-timesheet-golive]]).
- [ ] Agree the price: setup (scoped) + monthly retainer + hourly rate. Write it down.
- [ ] Decide hosting: his server vs our managed markup (affects blast radius + steward economics).
- [ ] **Send the invoice.** Get the first franc.

## Phase 1 — Turn Felix into a template + a payback story (1–3 months)
Finish his go-live, then **document the install as a playbook** (the seed of the Steward Onboarding Kit).
Felix = the reference + the before/after savings story. **Add zero features Felix doesn't need.**

## Phase 2 — One more, deliberately (3–6 months)
**One** complex maker-retailer from the sweet spot — winery / dairy / roastery / complex Hofladen. Not fifty.
The field map + tent card find the *serious* one; ignore the 366-excuses crowd on sight. Proves it works past
Felix and surfaces reusable-vs-custom. (This is where tiered/group pricing likely gets demand-pulled.)

## Phase 3 — Steward #1 (6–12 months)
Two live installs + a playbook + a savings story now exist. **Find one displaced IT person** (the archetype)
and teach them install #3 *with* you, then #4 *without* you. **This is the moment you stop being the ceiling.**
At 63 solo, not optional — it's the only way it scales or survives.

## Phase 4 — Let it compound (12–18 months+)
A few stewards, a dozen installs, modest recurring income = the **minimum-viable-income that compounds and
isn't all on your back.** For a solo 63-year-old, that IS winning — not a consolation prize.

## The rules that keep a solo 63yo alive
- **Demand-pull only** — build a feature when a *paying* customer needs it, never speculatively.
- **Say no** — to tire-kickers, scope creep, non-fit verticals. Energy is the budget, not time.
- **Externalize yourself** — every install → an SOP/recipe so the next needs *less* of you (scale + succession).
- **Cash-first** — if a task doesn't move toward a franc, it waits.
- **Keep the postcards running** — ISOTTO/print is low-effort cash *and* the door-opener to the shops. Don't
  drop the small money chasing the big.

## The age frame (strategy, not morbidity)
- **Continuity/legacy is real strategy.** The steward guild + the documented engine = something that can
  outlive you, be handed off, or sold. Design for hand-off from day one — the smart solo move.
- **CBD is the wedge, not the destination.** Use it to find serious shops; wineries/dairies/farms are the
  market; the steward guild is how you beat being one tired person.

## Scorecard / what "success" means here
NOT a unicorn. Success = **paying customers (start: 1), a repeatable playbook, 1–2 stewards, recurring income
that compounds and doesn't depend solely on Angel's hands.** Honest, modest, real.
