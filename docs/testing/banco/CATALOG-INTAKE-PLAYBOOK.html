🗂️ The Catalog Playbook — “Which Pile Is This?”Pick it up · zap it · know instantly whether it's 30 seconds or 15 minutes — and never fight one item 🐺 La Piazza · № LP-PLAYBOOK-20260717-CATALOG-INTAKE · rev.1 · for the Artemis shop

The whole idea: you do not treat every product the same. ~80% of the shop is a fast lane — zap it, confirm, done. ~20% is craft — slow on purpose. The skill isn't speed, it's knowing which pile something is in the second you pick it up, so you rip through the easy stuff and save your patience for the hard stuff. If an item doesn't fit the pile it should fit — stop. Put it aside. Something went wrong; that's a desk job, not a floor fight. Never burn 15 minutes on one thing at the counter.

1The one rule — pick it up, ZAP it

Every product starts the same way: scan the barcode. What happens next tells you the pile.

It rings up a product
✅ DONE — sell it (already catalogued)
“Not on file” but it has a barcode
① FAST LANE → find / adopt / enrich → save (born once)
The barcode won't read (curved can, glossy) after 2–3 tries
→ print OUR clean label → then Fast Lane
No barcode at all — generic glass, one-off
③ SLOW LANE → delivery slip → our photo/name/price + our label
No barcode — artisan line (jewelry, oils)
④ LINE solution → attributes + tiers, our label on display
It doesn't fit the pile it should, OR it's dragging past ~2–3 min
🅿️ PARK IT — desk job

3Our own labels — who gets one, what size, where it goes

The rule Felix needs to hear: we are NOT putting a barcode on a barcode. 90% already has one. We print our own only when there's none, or the printed one is unreadable (curved/glossy — after you've genuinely tried 2–3 times, not given up on the first zap).
SituationOwn label?Where it goes
Product already has a working barcodeNO— use the one that's there
Barcode won't read (curved can, tiny, glossy)YES — clean reprintFlat spot on the item, or on the shelf tag
No barcode — generic glass / one-offYESOn the item if flat; else on its box
Artisan display (oils, testers)YESOn the tester/display bottle so it can be scanned
Papers sold from an open display boxMaybe oneIn a catalog / label book at the till — not 50 stickers on 50 packs

Each product type has its own label size + placement — we lock those per type as we go, so it's the same every time.

4Artisan & jewelry — the “line,” not the piece

This is the trap: someone (Sylvie) comes in next week with 5 more unique pieces, and you're not spending 20 minutes photographing and naming every one. That's madness. Handle it as a line:

  • One product = the line (e.g. “Sylvie — Wool-Felt Glasses Case”), with options: colour (red/blue/black), all same size, all same price.
  • Jewelry that's just… jewelry → price tiers by look: the CHF 20 pieces, the CHF 30 pieces, the CHF 80 pieces. Pick the tier at the till; don't catalog each stone.
  • If it needs to scan → our own barcode on the display/tester, one per line or per tier.
  • No supplier catalog, so the maker's own photos + descriptions go in by hand (Cynthia's already looked great).

5Work it in sections — the shop already sorted itself

The shop is laid out in sections: all the papers here, all the trays there, all the CBD over there — the same product isn't scattered. Use that.

  • Do a whole section start to finish, then mark it DONE. Don't ping-pong across the shop.
  • A section is mostly one or two types (papers = A+B; glass = D) → you get into a rhythm and the type barely changes.
  • “Papers section: OCB + GIZEH ran clean, ELEMENTS parked for the desk. Section 90% done.” — that's the unit of progress.

6After the shelf is done — new stock comes through the back door

Once the store is catalogued, the job is small forever: only new products, and they all arrive the same way — in the back, with a delivery slip.

Catalog it at intake — before it hits the shelf.
The slip has the name, the article code, and the cost. That's the moment to do it right, cleanly. Never “put it on the shelf now, catalog it later.” That's exactly where the duplicates and the chaos start — the next person can't find it, makes a twin, and now you're patching at the till.

7What you're up against — set the expectation

  • The store carries well over 1,000 products — likely ~2,000. The supplier catalog behind it is 5,000–7,000. The catalog never truly “ends.”
  • But ~80% is the fast lane (types A–C) and can be secured systematically in days, not weeks.
  • The ~20% craft tail (D, E, F) is fine to be slow on — because you're not slow everywhere.
  • If you find yourself spending weeks or fighting every single item as its own special case — stop. That means the system broke down, not that the work is that hard. Re-sort into the piles above.
