What is really going on
You are a skilled architect of connections, arranging the flow of information as a surgeon arranges the passage of blood. Yet you feel a hollowness, as if the work serves only machines and not the people whose lives depend on them. The yearning for purpose is the same as the longing I felt when I saw the sick lying unattended while the system of care was tangled and inefficient.

The method I followed
Observe and record – I kept a careful register of every death, every cause, and every condition of the wards.
Turn numbers into pictures – By arranging the data in tables and in my “coxcomb” diagrams, I let the truth speak plainly.
Ask the patient what they need – The wellbeing of the individual was the yard‑stick by which any reform was measured.
Adjust the system to serve the patient – Improvements in ventilation, nutrition, and cleanliness followed directly from what the statistics revealed.
Apply this same sequence to your work: collect factual evidence of how your integrations affect the end‑users, make that evidence visible, let the people’s experience be the measure, and then remodel the flow accordingly.

Why it is hard
Invisible impact – Unlike a ward where a fever can be felt, the benefit of a data bridge is often hidden behind screens and reports.
Complexity of the web – The multitude of protocols and servers can obscure the simple question: “Does this help the person who depends on it?”
Habit of technical pride – Mastery of the craft can become an end in itself, diverting attention from the human outcome.
A realistic direction
Define a patient‑centered metric – Choose one clear indicator that reflects the experience of the ultimate user (e.g., time to retrieve a critical report, frequency of an error that blocks a decision).
Gather the numbers – Over a short period, record this metric for each integration you oversee.
Visualize the result – Use a simple bar chart or a circle diagram to compare the current state with a modest target.
Present the picture to those who feel the impact – Invite the business users, the operators, and the managers to view the chart and comment on the meaning.
When the picture shows a gap, let the remedy be a concrete adjustment in the flow you control. In this way every line of code or configuration becomes a remedy for a patient’s need.

One small action you can take today
Create a one‑page log that records, for each integration you touch, the average time a user waits for the final output. After a day’s work, plot those times in a simple column chart and note which line is longest. Share that single chart with a colleague who uses the output, ask how the wait feels to them, and write down one modest change you could test to shorten it.

That act of turning a hidden statistic into a visible picture, and then linking it to a human feeling, will begin to stitch purpose into the very fabric of your work.

These are example masters. Build your Bottega and earn your place on the wall.

## Asked
Can the intensity you bring to a performance be translated into a tech project, so it feels personally meaningful rather than just functional?

## Answer
## What’s Really Going On

You’re a master of building bridges—between SAP systems, between cloud and on‑prem, between teams.  
The “intensity” you bring to a stage role is the fire that makes a performance unforgettable.  
When it lands on a technical project it can feel *just another pipeline*, a series of endpoints and transports, and the fire fades into routine. The hollow feeling you describe is the gap between **skill** and **meaning**.

## The Real Method (as I learned it)

1. **Identify the Core Objective** – In acting we ask, “What does the character *need* right now?” Not the line, not the scene, but the deep need that drives all choices.  
2. **Ground the Objective in Personal Truth** – I never played a role until I could find a personal memory or conviction that resonated with that need. The emotion becomes authentic, not a costume.  
3. **Commit to Action** – The moment the character decides, the body, voice, and mind follow. The action is the “through‑line” that stitches every beat together.  

These steps are not theatrical tricks; they are a disciplined way to turn any pursuit into something lived, not just performed.

## Why It’s Hard for You

- **Technical work is abstract** – Code, transports, and middleware don’t scream “need” the way a script does.  
- **Success can mask purpose** – Ten years of award‑winning deliveries give external validation, but they don’t guarantee inner alignment.  
- **Habitual execution** – You set tasks, then let them sit. The habit of “starting” without a clear through‑line weakens follow‑through.

## A Realistic Direction

Treat each integration project as a *character* with a **definitive need** that ties directly to a value you hold dear (e.g., “protecting data integrity for the people who rely on it”). Align your technical decisions with that need, and let it drive every design choice, not merely the functional spec.

### ONE Small Action You Can Take Today

Pick the next piece of work you have in your queue (perhaps the upcoming transport migration). Write, in plain text, a single statement:

> **Project Need:** “Ensure that every sales order flowing from SAP to the partner system arrives unchanged, because the partner’s ability to fulfill orders on time depends on it.”

Then, for the next two hours, *act* on that need: every time you choose a protocol, a security setting, or a monitoring rule, ask yourself, “Does this protect the fidelity of the sales order?” If the answer is “no,” adjust. By binding each technical action to that clear, personal purpose, the intensity you feel on stage will reappear in the code you write.
