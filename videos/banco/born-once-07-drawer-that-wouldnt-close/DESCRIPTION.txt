Midnight. You count the till. It's forty francs short.

Not a disaster — but here's the thing that keeps an owner up at night: you have no idea where it went. A wrong change? A missed sale? A hand in the drawer? The old till just shrugs. One drawer, all day, every hand in it. The money is a blur. Three people worked today, and the shortfall has no name, no time, no story. So you sigh, you eat the forty francs, and you hope it's less tomorrow.

Banco does it differently. You start your shift by counting your float — your drawer, your number. From that second, every sale, every paid-in, every payout is tied to you. Not the till. You. At the end, you count the cash back, and the till tells you what it should be, to the franc. Expected. Counted. The difference, right there.

Now that forty francs isn't a mystery. It's Maria's drawer, Tuesday, the evening shift. Maybe an honest miscount. Maybe a coaching moment. Either way — you can see it. That's not suspicion. That's just light.

You can't fix what you can't see. Banco doesn't accuse anyone. It just counts — so you never have to wonder again. A drawer that closes, because it tells the truth.

Banco is a Swiss-made point-of-sale built for the counter, not the warehouse.
