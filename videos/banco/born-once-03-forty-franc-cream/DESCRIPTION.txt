Half the things in this shop never got a barcode.

Loose herbs, jars, lighters — and a cream that costs forty francs. The factory never gave it a barcode. So how does the new cashier sell it without a sticky note, a guessed price, or calling the manager every single time?

She doesn't scan. She taps the picture.

In Banco, every product wears its own face — a photo taken the first time it's ever sold. Born once, then known forever. So anyone can sell it by sight. A barcode belongs to the factory. A picture belongs to you — your shelf, your goods, your eyes.

Banco is a Swiss-made point-of-sale built for the counter, not the warehouse. You don't label the world before you can sell it. You sell what you can see.
