A box lands on the counter. Fifty tubes of the cream — the one that's been selling. The driver wants a signature. "Fifty units," he says. "Count 'em."

Ralph doesn't count.

He scans one tube — just to say "this arrived" — and he's done. He never types a quantity. Because here's the thing nobody tells you: Banco doesn't track how many you have. It tracks what you sell. The shelf isn't a number in a database — it's the real shelf, in the real shop.

That slip that says "5 trays"? That's not a stock count. That's how many labels to print. And the packaging knows the cost — you bought a box, it splits into singles, the margin's already there before the first one sells.

The delivery that used to eat twenty minutes and start an argument? Logged that it came, and walked away. No count. No reconcile. No clipboard. Dock to drawer — and nobody counted a thing.

Because you don't count a shop in. You sell it down.

Banco is a Swiss-made point-of-sale built for the counter, not the warehouse.
